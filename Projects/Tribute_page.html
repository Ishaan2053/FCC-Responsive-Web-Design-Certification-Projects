About Mahatma Gandhi

Mahatma Gandhi was a political leader, lawyer, and writer who fought for India's independence from British rule. He is widely considered to be one of the most influential leaders of the 20th century, and is commonly referred to as "Father of the Nation" in India.

Below are some of the key points of his life:

  • Born on October 2nd, 1869 in Porbandar, India
  • Studied law in London and later returned to India to practice law
  • Led the Indian National Congress in the Indian independence movement
  • Promoted non-violent civil disobedience as the means for social change
  • Led the Salt March in 1930, a non-violent protest against the British salt tax
  • Imprisoned multiple times by the British government for his activism
  • Played a key role in India's independence from British rule in 1947
  • Assassinated on January 30th, 1948 by Nathuram Godse
  • Remembered for his philosophy of non-violence and civil disobedience
  • Inspired civil rights and freedom movements around the world
  • Advocated for religious tolerance and women's rights
  • Promoted self-sufficiency and the use of village-based industries
  • Wrote numerous books, including his autobiography "The Story of My Experiments with Truth"
  • Received the Nobel Peace Prize in 1948
  • His birthday October 2nd is celebrated as Gandhi Jayanti, a national holiday in India
  • His non-violent movement influenced many including Martin Luther King Jr and Nelson Mandela
  • He was a vegetarian, and followed a strict code of non-violence
  • He believed in simple living, and advocated for the use of homespun cloth, and self-sufficient village economy
  • He was an advocate of "Swadeshi" philosophy, which called for the use of goods made in one's own country
  • His contributions to India's independence movement and philosophy of non-violence continue to be celebrated worldwide

Gandhi's Legacy

Mahatma Gandhi's legacy includes his non-violent philosophy of civil disobedience, which inspired civil rights and freedom movements around the world, including the American civil rights movement led by Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela's anti-apartheid movement in South Africa.


Mahatma Gandhi, also known as Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi, was a political and spiritual leader in India who played a key role in India's struggle for independence from British colonial rule. He is considered one of the most influential figures in modern Indian history and is often referred to as the "Father of the Nation." Gandhi's legacy is primarily based on his philosophy of nonviolence, which he called "satyagraha." He believed that peaceful resistance was the most effective way to achieve political and social change. He demonstrated this through his numerous campaigns of civil disobedience, including the non-cooperation movement in 1920 and the salt march in 1930. Gandhi's philosophy of nonviolence and civil disobedience inspired many leaders around the world, including Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela. His ideas on civil rights and freedom have had a lasting impact on the world and continue to be studied and admired today. Gandhi's legacy also includes his contributions to economic self-sufficiency and rural development. He advocated for village-based industries, small-scale agriculture, and the use of simple and locally available materials. Additionally, Mahatma Gandhi's teachings on simple living, self-reliance, and spiritual development have had a lasting impact on Indian society and continue to be an important part of the Indian culture and tradition. In conclusion, Mahatma Gandhi's legacy is one of nonviolence, civil disobedience, self-sufficiency, and spiritual development, which continue to inspire people around the world. His ideas and actions have had a lasting impact on India and the world.
